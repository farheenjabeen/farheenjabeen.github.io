My research builds imaging and multimodal models across cardiac MRI during my PhD and brain imaging and neural signals before it, with a focus on the problems that get in the way: limited labelled data, combining different kinds of medical data, and the variation between scanners, patients, and sites.

About

I recently completed my PhD at The University of Sheffield, UK, working with Prof. Richard H. Clayton and Dr. Chen (Cherise) Chen on deep learning for cardiac MRI. My PhD was funded by a Commonwealth Scholarship from the UK Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office. I passed my viva in 2026 and am awaiting formal conferral in August 2026.

My thesis, Towards Automated Segmentation of Cardiac Scars through Multimodal AI, automates the detection of myocardial scar from cardiac MRI, a task that normally takes a cardiologist considerable time and varies between readers. It comprises four frameworks, all developed and validated on real patient data from Nottingham University Hospitals NHS Trust.

The two I would point a reader to first are Seeing Beyond the Image and CLAIM. Seeing Beyond the Image fuses 12-lead ECG with LGE cardiac MRI through temporal-aware cross-attention, using the physiological signal to inform what imaging alone cannot show, and was selected for an oral at IEEE ISBI 2026. CLAIM is a conditional diffusion model that uses AHA-17 anatomical priors to synthesise realistic, diverse scar images for training, addressing the shortage of labelled data; it received the Best Paper Award at AIiH 2025. The remaining two are a cascaded multi-sequence segmentation network and a domain-generalised fusion transformer built to handle missing sequences and scanner variation.

Before Sheffield, I spent six years in research and teaching in Pakistan, after a Master's at the University of Engineering & Technology, Lahore, Pakistan on neuroimaging, where I worked on classifying Alzheimer's disease from fMRI. That is where my work in medical imaging began, and it carried through brain MRI and neural signals into the cardiac work that followed.

Research vision

My next direction is to take the generative and multimodal methods I built for cardiac imaging into the wider range of clinical problems that share the same constraints: scarce labels, multiple modalities, and data that varies across sites. The underlying principles carry over, anatomical and physiological conditioning, learning from limited data, and robustness across centres, and I am keen to apply them in new imaging domains and clinical questions, working closely with the people who will use the results.
