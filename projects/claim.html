TL;DR

We built a diffusion model that uses clinical heart anatomy as a conditioning signal, so synthetic scars are placed only where real scars physiologically can be. The result is a segmentation model that generalises to NHS patient data it has never seen.

The clinical problem

Why scar is so hard to see and so hard to label

Late Gadolinium Enhancement (LGE) MRI is the gold standard for visualising myocardial scar. Infarcted tissue accumulates gadolinium contrast, making it light up brightly against healthy muscle in the scan. Getting scar localisation right matters enormously: it directly guides arrhythmia risk stratification, ICD implantation, and ablation planning.

But the imaging challenge is real. Scar boundaries are poorly defined, soft-tissue contrast is low, and every patient looks different in terms of size, shape, location, and transmurality of the infarct. Even experienced cardiologists spend significant time on each case. Building large labelled datasets at scale is simply not feasible.

The gap

What existing synthesis methods get wrong

Conditional diffusion models for medical pathology synthesis have made real progress in recent years. But for cardiac scar, which is small, rare, and anatomically constrained, existing methods share three failure modes that matter:

Prior synthesis methods
👻 Data hallucination. Unintended alterations corrupt background regions. Non-scar anatomy is damaged by generation artifacts, which directly harms downstream segmentation quality.
⚖️ Biased diversity. Mask generation samples from training data statistics, inheriting their imbalances. Rare scar morphologies and locations stay consistently underrepresented.
🚫 No anatomical grounding. Generated scars can appear anywhere in the myocardium, including physiologically impossible locations that no real infarction could produce.
CLAIM (this work)
🛡️ Background preserved. Forward-diffused background is integrated at every reverse diffusion step, so non-scar anatomy is held fixed and remains untouched throughout generation.
🗺️ Atlas-driven diversity. Scar masks are generated from the AHA-17 atlas, sampling uniformly over location, size, and texture rather than from biased training statistics.
🫀 Clinical anatomy as a hard prior. Every synthetic scar respects AHA segment boundaries and coronary territory logic. Scars land only where real scars physiologically can.

The core module

SMILE: Scar Masks Incorporating cLinical knowledgE

SMILE is what makes CLAIM clinically grounded. It generates scar masks from the AHA 17-segment model, the standardised clinical map that divides the left ventricular myocardium into regions defined by both anatomy and coronary territory. This standard is used in every cardiology lab worldwide. To our knowledge, CLAIM is the first work to embed it inside a generative AI pipeline for cardiac scar synthesis.

The coronary territory logic is enforced as a hard constraint. LAD territory covers anterior and anteroseptal segments. RCA covers inferior and inferoseptal. LCx covers lateral. A synthetic inferior scar will not appear in a lateral segment. Full stop.

Step 01 · Register

Myocardium Fitting to AHA Atlas

Each subject's myocardium is registered to a template AHA-17 atlas using orientation correction, resampling, and rigid followed by non-rigid registration (fast symmetric forces demons).

Step 02 · Select

Location and Size via Adjacency Maps

One or more AHA segments are randomly selected from basal (1–6), middle (7–12), and apical (13–17) regions using adjacency maps to ensure coherence. Scar volume is sampled from a uniform distribution per region.

Step 03 · Generate

Textured Scar Blobs

3D scar blobs are generated per selected region with controlled anisotropy and porosity. Post-processing includes hole filling, Gaussian smoothing, and removal of isolated voxels for an anatomically plausible result.

Step 04 · Transform

Atlas Space to Subject Space

Scar masks are warped back to each subject's native anatomy using non-rigid and rigid registration, then composited onto the original myocardium. The result is ready to condition the diffusion model.

Architecture

The full CLAIM framework

CLAIM brings SMILE together with a conditional DDPM-based diffusion model (adapted from LeFusion) and a scar segmentation network built on nnUNet, trained alongside the generator. SMILE creates a diverse, anatomically constrained scar mask. The diffusion model synthesises a pathological LGE image conditioned on that mask, holding the background anatomy exactly in place. The segmentation network then trains on both real and synthetic data, and its feedback drives the generator toward more meaningful and realistic scar synthesis over time.

Quantitative results

9.4% better on data the model never saw

+9.40%
Dice improvement on out-of-domain data

The headline result is the out-of-domain gain. On 80 NHS patient scans from Nottingham (different scanner, different pixel spacing, never seen during training), CLAIM(J) improves Dice from 0.3284 to 0.4224. This is the hardest result to inflate and the most clinically meaningful: a model that actually generalises.

🏥
Real clinical data, NHS validated

The out-of-domain test set is 80 real LGE-CMR scans from Nottingham University Hospitals NHS Trust, labelled by cardiologists under formal ethics approval (NotIs CMR resource). This is not a held-out split of a public benchmark. It is a genuinely independent dataset with different scanner vendors and patient populations, making it a rigorous test of real-world clinical generalisability.

Clinical evaluation

Segment-by-segment: the clinical evaluation

Dice score is a standard computer vision metric. Clinicians care about a different question: how accurately is the volume of scar per heart region being estimated? Volume Difference (mL) per AHA-17 segment is a direct measure of clinical utility. Lower is better, and a tighter radar plot means more consistent accuracy across the whole ventricle.

CLAIM's radar plot is smaller and more uniform than the baseline's across all levels — basal, middle, apical, and apex. This matters in practice: large per-segment errors in specific coronary territories would make a tool unreliable for exactly the decisions it is meant to support, whether guiding ablation or planning device therapy.

Key contributions

Three things CLAIM adds to the field

🗺️01

First to embed AHA-17 into a deep learning generative framework for cardiac scar

SMILE conditions scar mask generation on the clinically adopted AHA 17-segment model for LV myocardium, ensuring every synthesised scar is spatially diverse, coronary-territory-constrained, and clinically interpretable. No prior generative model for cardiac pathology has incorporated this clinical standard into the AI training pipeline.

🔄02

Joint modelling paradigm for simultaneous synthesis and segmentation improvement

CLAIM trains a scar segmentation network alongside the diffusion generator in an alternating feedback loop. Segmentation feedback improves synthesis quality; synthetic data improves segmentation robustness. The two tasks are mutually reinforcing rather than sequential. CLAIM(J) + SMILE outperforms CLAIM + SMILE on both evaluation datasets.

🏥03

Robustness validated on 80 real NHS patient scans, not just a public benchmark

The out-of-domain evaluation uses 80 pathological LGE-CMR scans from Nottingham University Hospitals NHS Trust (different scanner, different pixel spacing, different patient population). CLAIM achieves +9.40% Dice over the baseline and surpasses the ICLR 2025 Spotlight comparison LeFusion + DiffMask, showing that anatomical conditioning holds up under real-world distribution shift.
